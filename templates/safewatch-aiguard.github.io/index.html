AutoPage

Human-Agent Collaborative Paper-to-Page Crafting for Under $0.1

Anonymous ACL submission

Introduction

In the pursuit of scientific progress, effectively communicating research is as crucial as the discovery itself. However, researchers are often burdened by the manual and repetitive task of creating project webpages to make their complex papers accessible. While automation has addressed static formats like slides and posters, the dynamic and interactive nature of webpages has remained an open challenge. To bridge this gap, we introduce AutoPage, a novel multi-agent system based on a collaborative, hierarchical process. AutoPage conceptualizes paper-to-page creation as a coarse-to-fine pipeline, spanning narrative planning, multimodal content generation, and interactive rendering. To counteract AI hallucination, dedicated 'Checker' agents verify each step against the source paper, and optional human checkpoints allow for feedback, transforming AutoPage into a powerful collaborative assistant. We also present PageBench, the first benchmark for this new task, which has rigorously validated our approach. AutoPage not only generates high-quality, visually appealing pages but does so efficiently, in under 15 minutes and for less than $0.1.

AutoPage Methodology

AutoPage is a multi-agent system designed to transform academic papers into interactive webpages through a coarse-to-fine, collaborative framework. As shown in Figure 2, its pipeline is structured into three main phases: Narrative Planning, Multimodal Content Generation, and Interactive Page Rendering. Throughout these phases, AutoPage employs two core principles: a verification mechanism at the conclusion of each stage to ensure factual grounding and optional human-in-the-loop checkpoints for flexible collaboration.

1. Narrative Planning and Structuring: This initial phase converts the PDF paper into a structured narrative blueprint. The Paper Content Parser ingests the document, converting it into a clean, JSON-like Markdown format using tools like MinerU and Docling. This creates an asset library containing text-based section summaries and visual representations (figures and tables with captions). The Page Content Planner then architects the webpage's high-level structure, devising a compelling narrative flow optimized for web presentation rather than a direct one-to-one mapping of the paper's original sections. This foundational blueprint undergoes a verification step to ensure completeness and logical soundness.

2. Multimodal Content Generation: Once the narrative blueprint is finalized, the Page Content Generator populates it with rich, multimodal content using a 'text-first' principle. The Text Content Generator synthesizes key information from the asset library into polished, human-readable paragraphs for each section. Subsequently, the Visual Content Generator analyzes this finalized prose to select and render the most relevant figures or tables, ensuring visual elements directly support the narrative. A two-stage verification and refinement process follows: an automated Content Checker verifies consistency between text and visuals, and optional Human-in-the-Loop Refinement allows authors to provide feedback for iterative content refinement.

3. Interactive Page Rendering: With author-approved content modules, the system renders them into a polished, interactive webpage. The Page Template Matcher allows users to select a template based on stylistic preferences (e.g., 'background_color', 'has_navigation') from a curated library. After content integration and interactive feature incorporation, the HTML Generator produces the final HTML, CSS, and JavaScript files. A crucial verification and customization stage includes an automated HTML Checker for layout and visual integrity, followed by optional Human-in-the-Loop mechanisms for authors to fine-tune styles via language commands. While AutoPage can operate autonomously, these interactive functionalities prioritize user control and flexibility, ensuring output aligns with the author's vision.

Overview of AutoPage. Throughout all phases, AutoPage integrates verification mechanisms and human-in-the-loop checkpoints for reliable and flexible generation. (2)

PageBench Benchmark

To rigorously evaluate AutoPage and foster future research, we developed PageBench, the first dedicated benchmark dataset for automated paper-to-page generation.

Dataset Curation: Our dataset is curated from over 1,500 project pages associated with papers from top-tier AI conferences (NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, 2023-2025). Each entry was meticulously filtered to ensure it constituted a valid project homepage. This benchmark is rich in multimodal content, including text, figures, and interactive demos.

Test Set and Template Library Construction: We employed a two-stage sampling strategy to ensure diversity. First, we extracted structural and stylistic features from our corpus, applied dimensionality reduction and clustering, and sampled 100 pages to form our primary test set, representing a broad range of page archetypes. Second, we deduplicated this test set to create a curated Template Library of 87 stylistically distinct pages.

Evaluation Metrics: To comprehensively assess webpage quality, we designed a suite of metrics covering two key dimensions: Content Quality and Visual Quality.

1. Content Quality:

* Readability: Measured by Perplexity (PPL) across all textual content, a lower score indicates higher linguistic fluency and coherence.

* Semantic Fidelity: Quantifies the semantic correspondence between generated webpage sections and their original paper paragraphs, using cosine similarity of vector embeddings after text alignment. Higher scores mean faithful preservation of meaning.

* Compression-Aware Information Accuracy: Evaluated via a question-answering (QA) pipeline. Questions are generated from the source paper, and answers are extracted from the generated webpage. The metric synthesizes answer factual correctness and text compression ratio, rewarding accurate and concise summaries.

2. Visual Quality (VLM-as-Judge framework): A VLM acts as a strict reviewer, assigning scores on a five-point scale for each dimension.

* Visual Content Accuracy: Assesses correct presentation of critical visual elements, focusing on mathematical formula rendering and contextual relevance of images to surrounding text.

* Layout and Cohesion: Evaluates structural integrity and visual balance, identifying flaws like disproportionate images or excessive white space to reward coherent and comfortable reading experiences.

* Aesthetic Score: Quantifies holistic visual appeal based on design harmony, color scheme coherence, style consistency, and clarity of the visual hierarchy, reflecting overall impression and professional polish.

Experimental Results

My experiments demonstrate that AutoPage significantly enhances the quality of academic project pages, both in content and visual presentation, consistently outperforming end-to-end methods. As shown in Table 1, integrating AutoPage consistently boosts both content and visual quality metrics across various base models, validating its effectiveness in producing accurate, coherent, and visually refined webpages. For instance, AutoPage-GPT4o-mini significantly improved the Aesthetic Score from 2.71 to 2.95 and Layout and Cohesion from 2.08 to 2.38 compared to the baseline GPT4o-mini. Similarly, AutoPage-Gemini-2.5-Flash achieved higher Semantic Fidelity (0.742 vs. 0.684) and Visual Content Accuracy (3.13 vs. 2.82), alongside a notable improvement in Compression-Aware Information Accuracy (1.276 to 1.941).

This mastery of the visual dimension is not just a quantitative improvement but translates directly into a superior user experience, as confirmed by our user study.

User Study and Discussion

To assess the human-perceived quality of the generated webpages, we conducted a user study with 20 participants. Using a forced-choice rating mechanism, participants assigned unique scores from 1 (Completely Unusable) to 10 (Perfect) to 8 webpages generated by different models for each source paper, compelling them to create a relative ranking.

Our user study demonstrated a significant preference for webpages generated by AutoPage. As shown in Figure 4, AutoPage achieved the highest average score of 7.16, establishing a clear performance hierarchy and maintaining a discernible advantage over other strong models like Grok4-fast (6.93) and Gemini-2.5-Flash (6.79). The substantial gap between AutoPage and lower-scoring models (e.g., GPT4o-mini at 3.97) underscores the task's difficulty and validates AutoPage's superior ability to align with human expectations.

Human preference study. The bar chart shows that AutoPage attains the highest user preference score, surpassing all baselines with more informative, coherent, and visually engaging webpages. (4)

Efficiency and Cost-Effectiveness: A critical aspect of AutoPage is its exceptional efficiency. Generating a complete, high-quality page costs between $0.06 and $0.20, with turnaround times ranging from 4 to 20 minutes depending on the chosen model. This remarkable cost-effectiveness confirms AutoPage as a scalable and economically viable solution.

Rethinking Readability Scores: While some baselines show good readability scores, our analysis in Table 1 reveals this can be misleading. For instance, GPT-4o-mini achieves a low PPL (10.047) but suffers from poor Semantic Fidelity (0.554). We argue that a low PPL, when coupled with inferior semantic fidelity and accuracy, does not indicate a truly sophisticated generation process, emphasizing the need for holistic evaluation.

Limitations and Future Work: While AutoPage shows promising results, several limitations guide future exploration: 1) PageBench currently focuses on ML conferences; extending coverage to other venues (ACL, CVPR, KDD) would enhance domain generality. 2) Our prototype relies on commercial API models, incurring costs and limiting reproducibility; future work could incorporate open-source models. 3) While our evaluation covers core aspects, additional user-centric studies are needed to provide a complementary perspective on usability and long-term impact.

Discussion

Efficiency and cost-effectiveness analysis A critical aspect of AutoPage is its exceptional efficiency in time and cost. Our experiments show that generating a complete, high-quality page costs between just $0.06 and $0.20, with turnaround times ranging from 4 to 20 minutes depending on the chosen model. This demonstrates a clear and valuable trade-off, allowing users to balance their specific needs for speed versus budget. This remarkable cost-effectiveness confirms that AutoPage is not merely a proof-of-concept, but a scalable and economically viable solution ready for real-world deployment.

Rethinking the readability Scores. While some baselines appear to excel in readability scores, our analysis of Tab. 1 reveals this can be a misleading indicator of true generation quality, often masking significant flaws. We observe the primary failure modes: sacrificing accuracy for fluency, as seen with GPT-4o-mini, which pairs a low PPL (10.047) with poor Semantic Fidelity (0.554), just better than llama-3.1-80B (0.442). We argue that a lower PPL, when coupled with inferior performance in semantic fidelity and accuracy metrics, is not an indicator of a more sophisticated, truly greater generation process, underscoring the need for a holistic evaluation beyond just readability.

Main Results
Experiment Setup
Baselines. Open-source: (1) most recent models specifically designed for guardrail tasks, i.e., LlavaGuard-34B, Holmes-VAD, and LLamaGuard3V-11B. (2) two powerful pre-trained MLLMs, i.e., InternVL2-8B and InternVL2-26B. Closed-source: the most advanced models available: GPT-4o, Gemini-1.5 Pro, and the Azure Video Content Moderation API.

Datasets. We evaluate and compare these guardrail models on (1) the high-quality test set of SAFEWATCH-BENCH-Real (830) and SAFEWATCH-BENCH-GenAI (590), covering both real-world and generative videos of six safety categories over 30 scenarios (2) Splits of five existing datasets, i.e., LSPD, XD-Violence, UCF, Fake-SV, FVC. (3) Generalizability to new policy categories: three unseen tasks during training, including children's safety, firearms, and road accidents.

Metrics. (1) Safety grounding, which denotes the ability to identify the correct policy violation in the video. This is measured by the accuracy (averaged per-category and per-split), F1 Score for multi-label prediction, and AUPRC by framing the guardrail task as a binary classification problem. (2) Explanation quality, which denotes the correctness and policy adherence of the guardrail explanations. We consider both GPT-4o as a judge and human evaluators, where we provide them with the video and the ground-truth response, and ask them to provide them to provide a rating ranging from 0 to 10. (3) Inference latency, which is measured by the average time (in seconds) between sending the guardrail request and receiving the response.
